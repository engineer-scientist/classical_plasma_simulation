Thanks. Is the C program creating physically accurate simulations? If not, is it because we are missing some physical laws? Or some other reason?


A.: 

Great question — and the honest answer is nuanced: the program is accurate for the model it actually implements, but that model is a deliberately simplified one, so it is not a first-principles "real plasma" simulation. The gap comes from both omitted physics and modeling/parameter choices — and for the runs you've done, the modeling choices matter more than the missing laws.

Let me separate the three layers.

1. What it gets genuinely right

Within its stated model — a box of classical, non-relativistic point charges under Coulomb + Lorentz forces — it is faithful:

- Coulomb interaction is real and direct. The O(N²) kernel sums the true pairwise electrostatic force between every pair. This is actually more accurate electrostatically than a typical grid-based PIC code (no grid smearing). The inter-particle "self-field" is present.
- Lorentz force q(E + v×B) and the Boris pusher are the industry-standard tools. We verified energy conservation (1e-4% to 0.03%), correct E×B drift, correct charge separation — the dynamics of each particle are trustworthy.

So the trajectories you've analyzed are correct for this model.

2. Physical laws that are missing (deliberate approximations)

These are real physics the code does not include:

- Full Maxwell / self-consistent fields. The magnetic field is external and static. Moving charges are currents that should generate their own B, and the interaction is treated as instantaneous Coulomb (no retardation, no propagation). This is the electrostatic + external-B approximation. Consequence: it can show electrostatic (Langmuir) oscillations but cannot support electromagnetic waves (light, Alfvén waves, etc.).
- No radiation. Accelerating charges (gyrating electrons, wall bounces) should emit Larmor radiation and lose energy — omitted. Small non-relativistically, but genuinely absent.
- No relativity. Purely Newtonian. Fine at 10⁴ K (v ≈ 0.001c), but in your strong-field B=0 run electrons were being accelerated hard — push Ex or T high enough and speeds would approach/exceed c with no relativistic correction.
- No quantum mechanics. No Pauli exclusion, no degeneracy, and — importantly — no recombination or atomic physics. The "softening length" is a numerical fudge standing in for the quantum short-range behavior; real recombination (e⁻ + p⁺ → H + photon) is a radiative quantum process that simply cannot happen here.
- No dissipation / thermostat. The system is isolated (microcanonical) with elastic walls — no energy exchange with an environment, no way to hold a fixed temperature.

3. Modeling & parameter choices (the bigger reason, for your runs)

These aren't "missing laws" — they're choices that limit how much the results resemble a real plasma:

- Sub-Debye, non-collective regime. This is the key one. Your runs had L/λ_D ≈ 0.14 (box smaller than the Debye length). Real plasma behavior — Debye shielding, proper wave dispersion, Landau damping — requires L ≫ λ_D and many particles per Debye sphere. At these parameters it's "a few thousand charged particles gyrating/bouncing in a box," not a collective plasma. (The code's own regime report flags this honestly.)
- Tiny N → huge discreteness noise. 2000 particles vs ~10²⁰ in reality. Statistical fluctuations and artificial collisionality are enormous. The slow numerical heating we saw (~2.5 K/ns) is largely this.
- Elastic specular walls. A lab idealization; real boundaries form sheaths / absorb particles. We saw this produce an oscillating charge separation (bouncing) instead of a realistic double-layer, and it introduces a small energy inconsistency when E ≠ 0.
- Softening modifies short-range physics. Necessary for stability, but it suppresses genuine large-angle Coulomb collisions, so the collision physics below ε ≈ 1 µm is not real Coulomb.
- Diagnostic caveat: T_kin mixes ordered bulk motion with random thermal motion (your "16 000 K" was mostly coherent electron sloshing), so it isn't a true thermodynamic temperature when there's flow.

None of this means the code is wrong — it's a well-constructed, self-aware classical model, and most educational (and many research) PIC codes make exactly the electrostatic + external-field approximation. It's just important to read its output as "correct classical mechanics of charged particles in a box," not "quantitative plasma physics."

If you want to move it toward genuine plasma realism, the highest-leverage changes, roughly in order, would be: (1) run in the collective regime (bigger box so L ≫ λ_D, more particles, ideally periodic boundaries instead of walls); (2) add a proper Coulomb collision model (or reduce softening and resolve close encounters); (3) make the fields self-consistent (a Poisson/PIC solve, or full EM-PIC for waves); (4) switch to the relativistic Boris push if you'll use strong fields. Want me to sketch or implement any of these — periodic boundary conditions and a proper collective-regime parameter set would be the most impactful and least invasive first step?
